Book Review

The Dark Side of Camelot by Seymour M. Hersh
⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️ (5/5)

Seymour M. Hersh’s The Dark Side of Camelot is not just a biography of John F. Kennedy — it is a much-needed counterbalance to decades of glorified storytelling, myth-making, and national nostalgia. In an era when public memory leans heavily on the romanticized vision of JFK’s “Camelot” years, Hersh dares to remind us that the light of that legendary court casts long, troubling shadows.

One of the most important lenses through which to read this book is as a balancing act. As readers, we are often too trusting when books present celebrated leaders in a glowing light. When Jackie Kennedy famously likened the White House years to a “brief shining moment from Camelot,” no one demanded documentation or independent verification. We welcomed the myth because it comforted us. Yet when Hersh paints an unflattering portrait of the same man — the instinctive response from many readers is to demand an unusual burden of proof.

Herein lies the irony: why should the requirement of evidence be greater when exposing flaws than when celebrating virtues? If we accept mythologizing without question, we should at least allow skepticism when someone attempts to crack that mythology — even if all the details cannot be triangulated with certainty. This, to me, is the greatest strength of The Dark Side of Camelot: it forces the reader to reckon with their own biases about leadership narratives.

Critics of the book often point to the improbability of some claims. But this skepticism overlooks a deeper truth: those who ascend to the highest echelons of power are not ordinary men living ordinary lives. The very path to political power often demands a shedding of conventional restraint and empathy. As Hersh implies — and history across cultures confirms — the environment of supreme authority can warp even the most ‘normal’ individuals into making decisions and behaving in ways that defy everyday logic. Thus, the burden of proof for ‘abnormal’ behavior need not be impossibly high; after all, abnormal circumstances can produce abnormal behavior without demanding abnormal proof.

Some of Hersh’s more serious charges — such as Kennedy’s willingness to prolong involvement in Vietnam purely to secure a second term, or his naive overconfidence in outmaneuvering the Russians to appear brilliant before his aides — are not only plausible but find supporting evidence in other credible historical sources. These claims align with broader patterns visible in other well-documented accounts of Kennedy’s decision-making.

However, the book’s reliance on unnamed or uncorroborated sources — particularly Secret Service agents whose testimonies are difficult to verify — is a legitimate weakness. This limitation is not to be dismissed lightly; responsible historical writing demands verifiability. But these gaps do not invalidate the book’s purpose or contribution. Rather, they highlight the inherent difficulty of breaking through decades of carefully curated political legend.

Despite these shortcomings, I believe The Dark Side of Camelot fully deserves a five-star rating. Why? Because Hersh succeeds in performing a rare service: challenging readers to view political leadership with skepticism rather than blind reverence. In a healthy democracy, this is not only valuable — it is essential. We need works like this to remind us that power corrupts, that myth is not history, and that truth is rarely pure or simple.

In conclusion, this book should not be dismissed for its uncomfortable revelations or imperfections in sourcing. Instead, it should be seen as part of the ongoing struggle to balance adulation with accountability, reverence with realism. Hersh’s book is an unsettling, provocative, and deeply necessary corrective to the legend of Camelot — and for that alone, it merits the highest praise.
