A Relentless Chronicle of Power, Press, and the Daily Struggle for Democracy

The Commissioner for Lost Causes by Arun Shourie
⭐️⭐️⭐️⭐️ (4/5)

Commissioner of Lost Causes is a compelling reminder that democracy is not self-sustaining — it must be defended every single day. Arun Shourie, through sharp reporting and fearless commentary, takes the reader into moments when India’s institutions began bending to the will of those in power.

The book chronicles significant episodes with depth and clarity: Bofors scandal, Mandal Commission, the Bhagalpur blindings of the early ’80s, the Bihar Press Bill under, Ashwin Sarin’s women trafficking exposé, Chief Minister Abdul Rahman Antulay’s trusts, the murky $175 million Kuo oil deal pushed through successive petroleum ministers, the 1988 Defamation Bill that sparked a renewed fight for press freedom, and the Postal Bill that passed with a mere 20 MPs present. The Thakkur commission report’s suspicion on officials in Mrs.Gandhi's assasination also finds a place in these pages.

One of the book’s strengths is how it exposes the uneasy dependence of Indian newspapers on government advertising — a structural weakness that makes sustained criticism of government policy rare.

The anecdotes, backed by Shourie’s characteristic wit and indignation, are punctuated with powerful lines such as, “The official secrets act was the last refuge of every crook” and, “Our (journalist's) work must be such that, when the powerful silence us, the reader must feel that his tongue has been yanked out.” Perhaps most memorable is Shourie’s Magsaysay Award speech, where he observes that “parasites have become rulers” and that the real tragedy is not the misuse of power by rulers, but the failure of citizens to use their own power to stop them.

I’m holding back from giving it a full 5 stars because at times the prose could have been tighter — an editor’s hand might have trimmed repetitions and made the narrative even more impactful.
