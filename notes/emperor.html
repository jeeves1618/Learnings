  • Cancer: A form of pathological hyperplasia where cells acquire an autonomous will to increase.

Retroviruses and Cancer

Some viruses can create DNA out of RNA. These viruses are called retroviruses.

Oncogene

An oncogene is a gene that has the potential to cause cancer. It arises from a mutation or overexpression of normal genes called proto-oncogenes, which play a key role in regulating cell growth and division. When these proto-oncogenes become altered, they can lead to uncontrolled cell proliferation, resulting in tumor formation.

Key Points about Oncogenes:

  • Normal Role (Proto-oncogenes): Regulate normal cell growth and differentiation.
  • Mutation & Activation: When mutated or overexpressed, proto-oncogenes turn into oncogenes.
  • Effect on Cells: Promotes unchecked cell division, avoiding apoptosis (natural cell death).
  • Causes of Activation: Genetic mutations, chromosomal translocations, viral infections, or environmental factors like carcinogens and radiation.

How Oncogenes Contribute to Cancer:

  • Bypass normal growth control mechanisms, leading to tumor formation.
  • Often work with other genetic mutations (e.g., tumor suppressor gene loss) to drive cancer progression.
  • Targeted therapies, such as HER2 inhibitors (Trastuzumab) or BCR-ABL inhibitors (Imatinib), are designed to block oncogene activity.

Cytotoxic Chemotherapy

Cytotoxic chemotherapy is a type of cancer treatment that uses drugs to kill or damage rapidly dividing cells, including cancer cells. The term "cytotoxic" means toxic to cells, and chemotherapy drugs work by interfering with cell division, ultimately leading to cell death (apoptosis) or stopping cells from multiplying.

How Cytotoxic Chemotherapy Works

Cancer cells divide uncontrollably, and cytotoxic chemotherapy targets this process. It primarily affects cells in the mitotic phase (actively dividing). However, it does not distinguish between cancerous and normal rapidly dividing cells, leading to side effects.

Mechanisms of Action

  • Alkylating Agents (e.g., Cyclophosphamide) - Damage DNA to prevent cell division.
  • Antimetabolites (e.g., Methotrexate, 5-Fluorouracil) - Mimic natural substances in DNA and RNA synthesis.
  • Topoisomerase Inhibitors (e.g., Doxorubicin, Etoposide) - Interfere with DNA unwinding enzymes.
  • Mitotic Inhibitors (e.g., Paclitaxel, Vincristine) - Disrupt microtubules, preventing mitosis.
  • Cytotoxic Antibiotics (e.g., Doxorubicin, Bleomycin) - Cause breaks in DNA strands.

Side Effects

  • Bone marrow suppression - Low blood cell counts (anemia, infections, bleeding).
  • Gastrointestinal issues - Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea.
  • Hair loss (Alopecia) - Due to damage to hair follicle cells.
  • Reproductive effects - Potential infertility risks.

Usage in Cancer Treatment

  • Curative intent - Eliminating cancer (e.g., leukemia, lymphoma).
  • Adjuvant therapy - Used after surgery/radiation to kill residual cells.
  • Neoadjuvant therapy - Given before surgery to shrink tumors.
  • Palliative care - Slowing progression in advanced cases.

Advancements and Alternatives

While cytotoxic chemotherapy is effective, newer treatments such as targeted therapies, immunotherapy, and hormone therapy are being developed to reduce side effects and improve outcomes.

The Core Idea

Is the end of cancer conceivable in the future? Is it possible to eradicate this disease from our bodies and our societies forever?

The answers to these questions are embedded in the biology of this incredible disease. Cancer, we have discovered, is stitched into our genome. Oncogenes arise from mutations in essential genes that regulate the growth of cells. Mutations accumulate in these genes when DNA is damaged by carcinogens, but also by seemingly random errors in copying genes when cells divide. The former might be preventable, but the latter is endogenous. Cancer is a flaw in our growth, but this flaw is deeply entrenched in ourselves. We can rid ourselves of cancer, then, only as much as we can rid ourselves of the processes in our physiology that depend on growth—aging, regeneration, healing, reproduction.

Science embodies the human desire to understand nature; technology couples that desire with the ambition to control nature. These are related impulses—one might seek to understand nature in order to control it—but the drive to intervene is unique to technology. Medicine, then, is fundamentally a technological art; at its core lies a desire to improve human lives by intervening on life itself. Conceptually, the battle against cancer pushes the idea of technology to its far edge, for the object being intervened upon is our genome. It is unclear whether an intervention that discriminates between malignant and normal growth is even possible. Perhaps cancer, the scrappy, fecund, invasive, adaptable twin to our own scrappy, fecund, invasive, adaptable cells and genes, is impossible to disconnect from our bodies. Perhaps cancer defines the inherent outer limit of our survival. As our cells divide and our bodies age, and as mutations accumulate inexorably upon mutations, cancer might well be the final terminus in our development as organisms.
