Favorite Parts from "Age of AI"

  • The process undergone by artificial intelligence to acquire knowledge and capability is called machine learning.
  • While the advancement of AI may be inevitable, its ultimate destination is not.
  • AI promises to transform all realms of human experience. At its core, this transformation will occur at the philosophical level, changing how humans understand reality and our role within it.
  • Artificial intelligence is altering the human relationship with reason and reality. This is a revolution for which existing philosophical concepts and social institutions leave us largely unprepared.
  • Artificial intelligence does not possess self-awareness – in other words, the ability to reflect on its role in the world.
  • The conviction that what we see reflects reality — and that we can fully comprehend at least aspects of this reality using discipline and reason — inspired the Greek philosophers to great achievements.
  • Aided by advancements in printing technology, the leaders of the Protestant Reformation declared individuals are capable of — indeed, responsible for — defining the divine for themselves.
  • The Reformation validated the possibility of individual faith existing independent of church arbitration. From that point forward, received authority became subject to the probing and testing of autonomous inquiry.
  • The Reformation and Renaissance inspired new inquiries into the underlying mechanisms of the natural world. This exploration of historical knowledge, and an increasing sense of agency over the mechanisms of society, also inspired geographic exploration.
  • This exploration resulted in confrontations with a new aspect of reality: societies with different gods, divergent histories, and independently developed forms of economic, achievement, and social complexity. Such experiences began to broaden the aperture of the Western mind.
  • When scientific advancements met theological bedrock, the outcome was incongruence: societies remained united in their monotheism but divided by competing interpretations and explorations of reality. Societies needed a concept — indeed, a philosophy — to guide their quest to understand the world and their role in it. The philosophers of Enlightenment answered the call, declaring reason both the method and the purpose for interacting with the environment.
  • The minds that set out on the journey of reason were willing to walk a precarious path, risking the apparent certainty of their cultural traditions and their established conceptions of reality.
  • In his work "Critique of Pure Reason," Kant argued that:
    • We do not perceive the world “as it is” (the noumenon), but as it appears to us (the phenomenon) through our own mental faculties.
    • He claimed that the human mind actively shapes experience by structuring it through categories like time, space, and causality.
  • According to Immanuel Kant, while the human mind might present an imperfect picture of reality, it is the only picture available. This lack of an alternative to the human mind is resolved with the arrival of artificial intelligence. AI is beginning to provide an alternative means of accessing — and thus understanding — reality.
  • Austrian philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein said that knowledge wants to be found in generalizations about similarities across phenomena. He termed this "family resemblances." Later, in the 21st century, his ideas influenced artificial intelligence and machine learning. His theories posited that AI’s potential lay partly in its ability to scan large data sets to learn types and patterns.
  • Even if AI would never know something in the way a human mind could, an accumulation of matches with the patterns of reality could approximate — and sometimes exceed — the performance of human perception and reason.
  • As Kant predicted, humans have interpreted the world according to the structure of their own minds. But as humans began to approach the limits of their cognitive capability, they became willing to enlist machines to augment their thinking in order to transcend their cognitive limitations.
  • With AI, we have reached a tipping point: we can no longer conceive of some of our innovations as extensions of what we already know.
  • Information is not self-explanatory. It is context-dependent. To be useful, or at least meaningful, it must be understood through the lenses of culture and history. In other words, when information is contextualized, it becomes knowledge.
  • The internet inundates users with the opinions of thousands — even millions — of other users, depriving them of the solitude required for sustained reflection that, historically, has led to the development of convictions. As solitude diminishes, so too does fortitude — not only to develop convictions but also to be faithful to them, particularly when they require traveling novel, and thus often lonely, roads.
  • Programming to ML: We went from attempting to encode human-distilled insights into machines to delegating the learning process itself to the machines. Learned rather than directly specified. The formalistic and inflexible systems — successful only in domains whose tasks could be achieved by encoding clear rules based on human insights — got replaced by indeterministic and flexible artificial intelligence systems.
  • Why do we need machine learning: the variability and subtlety of the real world do not reduce to simple rules.
  • To translate one language to another, a translator needs to capture specific patterns: sequential dependencies.
  • Human perception and experience filtered through reason have long defined our understanding of reality. With the advent of AI, the individual human mind is no longer reality's sole or perhaps even its principal navigator.
  • Today’s near-constant stream of media increases the cost and decreases the frequency of contemplation. Algorithms promote what seizes attention in response to the human desire for stimulation, and what seizes attention is often the dramatic, the surprising, and the emotional. Whether an individual can find space in this environment for careful thought is one matter. Another is that the now dominant forms of communication are non-conducive to the promotion of tempered reasoning.
  • Examples like AlphaZero, AlphaGo, AlphaFold, and the creation of Halicin antibiotic show that there are ways of knowing that are not available to human consciousness. To expand the frontiers of contemporary knowledge, we may ask AI to probe realms we cannot enter; it may return with parameters or predictions we do not fully grasp.
  • Accessed by AI, new horizons are opening before us. Previously, the limits of our minds constrained our ability to aggregate and analyze data, filter, process news and conversations, and interact socially in the digital domain. AI permits us to navigate these more effectively. In doing so, it not only expands physical reality but also the pyramid, expansion, and organization of the burgeoning digital world.
  • Yet at the same time, AI subtracts. It hastens dynamics that erode human reason as we have come to understand it: social media, which diminishes the space for reflection, and online searching, which decreases the impetus for conceptualization. As deep reading and analysis contract, so too do the traditional rewards for undertaking these processes.
