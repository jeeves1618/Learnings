Beyond Entrepreneurship 2.0

Staying in a corporate job is like a paint-by-numbers kit approach to life, where everything is laid out for you, and as long as you stay within the lines, you are more or less guaranteed to have a nice picture at the end.

If you spend your life keeping your options open, that is exactly what you will do—spend your life keeping your options open.

The Right People

The right people display window and mirror maturity. When things go well, the right people point out the window, giving credit to factors other than themselves. They shine a light on other people who contributed to the success and take little credit themselves.

And when things go wrong, they don’t blame circumstances or other people for the setbacks and failures; they point in the mirror and say, "I am responsible."

Responsibilities Over Jobs

The right associates understand that they don’t have jobs; they have responsibilities. They grasp the difference between their task list and their true responsibilities. A great doctor doesn’t merely have the job of performing procedures but embraces responsibility for the health of the patient.

Leadership

Most exceptional leaders grow into their capabilities, not because they want to be a great leader, but because they are trying to be worthy of the people they lead.

The cellular structure of any truly great organization is the well-led unit, for this is where great things get done. Great leadership at the top doesn’t amount to very much without exceptional leadership at the unit level.

Effective Leadership

Effective leadership has two components: function and style. Leadership is the art of getting people to want to do what must be done.

Speaking Up

As a member of this engineering team, you have not only the opportunity to engage in the dialogue, you have the responsibility to do so. If you fail to advance your argument, if you fail to disagree with the most powerful person in the room, if you fail to bring solid logic and evidence to the debate, then you are failing in that responsibility.

Time Management

There is a vital difference between managing time and managing work: work is infinite; time is finite. Work expands to fill whatever time is allocated to it. To be productive, therefore, you must manage your time, not your work. The key question to ask yourself is not “What am I going to do?” but “How am I going to spend my time?”

Leadership Styles

A micromanager doesn’t trust his people and seeks to control every single detail and decision; he believes that ultimately only he will make the right choices. A personal-touch leader, on the other hand, trusts his people to make fundamentally good choices and respects their abilities.

Constructive Criticism

When things don’t go well, don’t criticize the person. Just examine the events.

Business and Profit

Profit, like breathing, is indispensable. But it is not the sole goal of our business.

Great Business vs. Bad Business

How do you know if you have a truly great business? Warren Buffett has the best answer: “You don’t have to hold a prayer meeting to raise prices.” If you cannot control prices, you have to control costs.

How do you know if you have a truly bad business? You are too small to take advantage of cost economies of scale yet not differentiated enough to justify higher prices than the competition. You are stuck in the middle, and it is deadly.
